"A steam trap is a device for allowing the passage of condensate and preventing the passage of steam, or for allowing the passage of air as well as condensate. Common steam trap types include float and thermostatic (F&T) traps, inverted-bucket traps, and thermodynamic traps." - ASHRAE terminology

When condensate gets collected it reduces the heat transfer efficiency and can cause water hammer effect.

Steam trap types Operation driven by Advantages Disadvantages Application
Float and thermostatic
  • The difference in temperature between steam and sub-cooled condensate
  • Allows rapid purging of air at startup
  • Can handle high back pressure and large air quantities
  • Unable to handle dirt
  • Process application (removing condensate from a heat transfer process)
  • When large quantity of air is present in the system
Mechanical (inverted-bucket)
  • the difference in condensate and steam density
  • Robust
  • Resistant to water hammer
  • Resistant to dirt and scale
  • Unable to distinguish difference in steam and air density
  • Some steam gets purged out with air
  • Not suitable for removing large quantities of air
  • Drip applications on steam main and supply lines
Thermodynamic (disc type)
  • the differences in pressure applied by the steam and condensate
  • Resistant to water hammer
  • Anti-freezing
  • Not suitable for removing large quantities of air
  • Unable to handle dirt
  • Poor operation against back pressure
  • Drip applications on steam main and supply lines

Operation and Training Resources

Steam traps need to be tested periodically for cold plugging (blocked condensate discharge or insufficient capacity) and failing in open position such that the steam is escaping into the condensate return system. The three common ways of testing steam trap failures are:

  • Temperature: Works best to identify cold plugging
  • Sound: Different sound types for leaking and blocked traps; electronic/ultrasonic instruments are used for greater accuracy
  • Visual observation: Condensate not flowing or steam escaping with condensate.

For more information on instrumentation and steps for identifying steam trap failures see:

  • A Guide to Steam Trap Testing , TLV Company
  • Steam Trap Testing Guide for Energy Conservation, Armstrong
  • Best Practices for Ultrasound Testing of Steam Traps, Plant Engineering

Case Studies

Steam Trap Performance Assessment at Veterans Administration (VA) medical centers
Three hospitals located in Providence, Rhode Island, and Brockton and West Roxbury, Massachusetts, were inspected and evaluated to estimate stream trap losses due to malfunctioning. Based on different steam pressures and end uses, steam losses were estimated at $5.25 per 1,000 pounds of steam at Providence and $4.25 per 1,000 pounds of steam at Brockton and West Roxbury. The annual estimated savings was $18,695 at Providence, $70,511 at Brockton, and $3,117 at West Roxbury.
